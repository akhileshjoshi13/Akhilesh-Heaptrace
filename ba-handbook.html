Why This Handbook Exists

The Problem We Solve

Variance is the ultimate failure mode. A BA without a strict process becomes the bottleneck between client intent and developer execution. When two BAs on the same team produce wildly different quality outputs — the client loses trust, the developers lose time, and the project loses money.

This handbook completely eliminates that variance — regardless of your seniority level, domain, or client type. Every process, every template, every AI skill is standardised here. If you follow this handbook, a Junior BA produces Senior-BA-quality output. That is the only acceptable standard at Heaptrace.

Principle 01
" A requirement without a testable acceptance criterion is a wish, not a requirement.
Principle 02
" The BA is not a note-taker. The BA is the contract between the client's intent and the developer's output.
Principle 03
" If the developer had to ask a question the BRD should have answered, the BA failed at their job.
Principle 04
" Scope creep without a change request is a gift to the client and a debt to the project.

01
Discovery & Scoping
Objective: Understand the problem, define the boundary, align all stakeholders
Scoping Document Stakeholder Map Open Questions Log Project Plan Draft
This is the most critical phase of any engagement. Every assumption you make here becomes a risk later. The goal is not to start building — it is to fully understand what you are being asked to build, for whom, and within what constraints. Do not leave this phase until the entire team is aligned on scope.
What Happens
  • Conduct initial client meetings to understand the business problem and desired outcome
  • Identify and map all stakeholders — decision-makers, approvers, users, and influencers
  • Define the project scope boundary — what is in, what is explicitly out
  • Document all assumptions being made about the project at this stage
  • Create the first open questions list with named owners and deadlines
  • Assess the competitive landscape and existing solutions in the client's space
  • Identify integration touch points, third-party dependencies, and legacy systems
  • Conduct a high-level compliance and regulatory assessment for the domain
  • Agree on the communication cadence, escalation path, and named client approver
  • Produce the initial project timeline skeleton with key milestones
Key Deliverables
  • Scoping document with in-scope / out-of-scope breakdown
  • Stakeholder register with roles, authority levels, and contact details
  • Open questions log — each item with owner and resolution deadline
  • High-level project milestone plan
  • Assumptions and constraints register
  • Initial risk register (scope, timeline, compliance, integration risks identified)
Common Pitfalls
  • ⚠ Leaving discovery with verbal agreements only — nothing documented
  • ⚠ Not identifying the single named approver before moving to Phase 2
  • ⚠ Treating a one-hour intro call as sufficient discovery
  • ⚠ Not logging out-of-scope items — leads to scope creep arguments later

02
Requirement Gathering
Objective: Extract, validate, and structure all business and functional requirements
Workshop Notes Requirements List User Personas Process Flows
Requirements are not collected — they are uncovered. Clients rarely know everything they need on day one. This phase is iterative: run workshops, send follow-up questions, validate understanding, and run workshops again. A requirement you miss here will become a Change Request mid-sprint.
What Happens
  • Run structured discovery workshops with business stakeholders — functional and technical
  • Conduct one-on-one interviews with end users to understand their actual workflows
  • Document functional requirements (what the system must do) and non-functional requirements (how it must perform)
  • Map existing business processes and identify pain points being solved
  • Define user personas and use cases for each user type
  • Capture data requirements — what data exists, what needs to be created, what needs to migrate
  • Identify all third-party integrations, APIs, and external systems involved
  • Run a compliance and regulatory pass — HIPAA, GDPR, PCI-DSS, etc. as applicable
  • Review and close all open questions from Phase 1
  • Validate requirements back with stakeholders before moving to BRD drafting
Key Deliverables
  • Structured requirements list (functional + non-functional + assumptions)
  • Workshop notes and meeting minutes sent to client same day
  • User personas and role-based use cases
  • As-is process maps showing current workflows
  • Data dictionary and migration scope (if applicable)
  • Updated open questions log with all Phase 1 items resolved
Common Pitfalls
  • ⚠ Running only one workshop and treating it as complete discovery
  • ⚠ Capturing requirements only from the most vocal stakeholder
  • ⚠ Not documenting non-functional requirements (performance, security, scalability)
  • ⚠ Skipping compliance check at this phase — too late after FRD is written

5Use AI to draft the open questions list from any gaps found, with suggested owners and priority levels.

03
BRD Creation & Sign-off
Objective: Translate raw requirements into a structured, client-approved BRD
BRD v1.0 Client Sign-off Revised Estimates
The BRD is the business-level agreement. It is written in language the client can understand, approved by the person with authority to approve it, and it becomes the commercial anchor for the rest of the engagement. A BRD without a formal sign-off is not a BRD — it is a draft.
What Happens
  • Consolidate all Phase 2 requirements into a structured BRD document
  • Write the executive summary, business context, and problem statement
  • Structure requirements into logical modules or business domains
  • Document all assumptions, constraints, and dependencies explicitly
  • Include compliance and regulatory requirements as a dedicated section
  • Share BRD draft with client for review — track all comments and queries
  • Run a BRD walkthrough session with key stakeholders to address questions
  • Iterate on the document based on client feedback — version-control every change
  • Obtain formal written sign-off from the named client approver
  • Update the project plan and effort estimate based on confirmed BRD scope
Key Deliverables
  • BRD v1.0 — fully reviewed and version-controlled
  • Formal written sign-off from named client approver (email or signed document)
  • BRD review meeting notes and resolved feedback log
  • Updated project plan reflecting confirmed BRD scope
  • Revised effort estimate and commercial confirmation if scope changed
Common Pitfalls
  • ⚠ Treating a verbal "yes" on a call as formal BRD sign-off
  • ⚠ Sending BRD to the wrong person — approval must come from the named authority
  • ⚠ Moving to FRD while BRD open questions are still unresolved
  • ⚠ Not versioning the document — teams argue over which version was approved

04
FRD Development
Objective: Define every system behaviour in enough detail that development can begin without ambiguity
FRD Draft User Stories Data Models API Contracts
The FRD is the technical contract. Where the BRD says "what" and "why", the FRD says "how" — every screen, every field, every workflow, every edge case. A developer reading the FRD should be able to build without a single follow-up question. If they need to ask, the FRD is incomplete.
What Happens
  • Break the BRD into functional modules and assign each to a section of the FRD
  • Write detailed user stories with personas, goals, and Given/When/Then acceptance criteria
  • Document every screen, form, and UI component with field-level specifications
  • Define data models, entity relationships, and validation rules
  • Specify all API contracts — endpoints, request/response schemas, error handling
  • Document business rules, calculation logic, and conditional workflows
  • Map all edge cases and error states — not just the happy path
  • Set up and prioritise the product backlog in Jira from FRD content
  • Conduct Sprint 0 — environment setup, architecture review, team briefing
  • Maintain an open questions log — close every item before FRD sign-off
Key Deliverables
  • FRD draft — module by module, version-controlled
  • User stories with full acceptance criteria in Jira
  • Data model / ERD diagrams
  • API contract specifications
  • Prioritised product backlog in Jira
  • Sprint 0 completion — dev environment ready
Common Pitfalls
  • ⚠ Writing FRD sections without edge cases — they always exist
  • ⚠ Creating Jira tickets before the FRD is reviewed — leads to rework
  • ⚠ Not getting tech lead input on feasibility before FRD is finalised
  • ⚠ Leaving integration specifications vague — biggest cause of sprint blockers
🤖 AI Process — How to Use AI in This Phase
1Feed the approved BRD into pm-delivery-engine and request an FRD. It will generate functional specs, screen-level descriptions, data flows, and an open questions log.
2For each functional requirement ask AI: "What are the edge cases and error states for this feature?" — add all to the FRD.
3Run the FRD through compliance-manager — it will flag any HIPAA, GDPR, DPDP, or OWASP gaps in the functional design.
4Ask AI to generate the non-functional requirements section: performance SLAs, uptime targets, security requirements, scalability assumptions.
5Use AI to cross-check: "Does every requirement in the BRD have a corresponding functional spec in this FRD?"
⚡ Benefits
✓FRD covers edge cases a manual process would miss.
✓100% BRD-to-FRD traceability verified by AI in seconds.
✓Non-functional requirements never forgotten — AI generates them systematically.
✓Compliance built into the FRD from day one, not bolted on later.

05
FRD Sign-off & Sprint Kickoff
Objective: Lock scope, get written approval, and launch the first sprint with the team fully aligned
Signed FRD Sprint 1 Plan Team Briefing Done
FRD sign-off is the commercial and technical gate before development begins. Once signed, scope is locked. Any changes after this point require a formal Change Request. The entire delivery team must be briefed on the FRD before the first sprint starts — no developer should write a line of code without having read the relevant FRD sections.
What Happens
  • Share finalised FRD with client for formal review — set a defined review deadline
  • Run an FRD walkthrough session covering all modules, edge cases, and open items
  • Resolve all client feedback — document every change made during review
  • Obtain written sign-off from named client approver on the final FRD version
  • Communicate to the entire team that scope is now locked — any change needs a CR
  • Brief every developer and QA member on FRD sections relevant to their work
  • Finalise Sprint 1 plan — stories, capacity, goal, and success definition
  • Confirm delivery milestones, sprint cadence, review schedule with the client
  • Set up the project communication structure — weekly status, sprint reviews, escalation path
Key Deliverables
  • FRD — formally signed off in writing, version-locked
  • Sprint 1 plan — stories committed, capacity confirmed
  • Team briefing notes — confirming everyone has read their FRD sections
  • Delivery calendar — sprint dates, review dates, milestone dates
  • Change Request process communicated and agreed with client
Common Pitfalls
  • ⚠ Starting Sprint 1 before FRD is formally signed — the most expensive mistake in delivery
  • ⚠ Not briefing the full dev team — leads to different interpretations of requirements
  • ⚠ Not communicating the CR process — client assumes changes are free
  • ⚠ Overcommitting Sprint 1 — first sprint velocity is always lower than expected

06
Sprint Delivery
Objective: Deliver working software iteratively against the signed FRD, sprint by sprint
Working Software Sprint Reports CR Log Risk Updates
Sprint delivery is where the project lives or dies. It is not just about writing code — it is about maintaining alignment between what was agreed, what is being built, and what the client expects. Every deviation from the FRD must be caught immediately and handled through the formal CR process. Silence is not delivery.
What Happens
  • Run sprint ceremonies every sprint: planning, daily standup, sprint review, retrospective
  • Clarify acceptance criteria for stories as they enter development — zero ambiguity
  • Track sprint velocity and burndown — flag early if sprint goal is at risk
  • Review and groom the backlog at least once per sprint — reprioritise as needed
  • Log and assess every client change request against the signed FRD
  • Issue formal CRs for any out-of-scope request — get commercial decision before starting work
  • Send weekly status updates to the client — RAG status, progress, risks, blockers
  • Conduct sprint demo with the client at end of every sprint — get feedback on the record
  • Update the risk register weekly — escalate any MEDIUM or higher risk immediately
  • Maintain a defect log — classify every defect as in-scope or out-of-scope against the FRD
Key Deliverables
  • Working software demo at end of each sprint
  • Sprint review notes — sent to client after every demo
  • Weekly RAG status report
  • Active CR log — every scope change tracked and commercially resolved
  • Updated risk register — reviewed and actioned weekly
  • Backlog updated after each sprint retro
Common Pitfalls
  • ⚠ Absorbing scope changes without a CR — the leading cause of fixed-price overruns
  • ⚠ Not escalating blockers within 48 hours — small blockers become project stoppers
  • ⚠ Skipping sprint reviews — client surprises at UAT are always avoidable
  • ⚠ Losing visibility of velocity trends — late detection of delivery risk

07
QA & Internal Testing
Objective: Validate every FRD acceptance criterion before the client ever touches the system
Test Results Defect Log QA Sign-off
QA is not the first time quality is checked — it is the last internal gate before the client sees the product. If something is found in QA that should have been caught during development, the acceptance criteria were not clear enough. QA validates the product; requirements documents define what QA tests against.
What Happens
  • Execute test cases against every acceptance criterion in the FRD — happy path and edge cases
  • Perform functional testing: all features, workflows, and business rules
  • Perform integration testing: all third-party connections, APIs, and data flows
  • Perform non-functional testing: performance benchmarks, security checks, browser/device compatibility
  • Log every defect with severity, steps to reproduce, and FRD reference
  • Classify each defect: in-scope (Heaptrace fixes) vs out-of-scope (CR required)
  • Track defect resolution — no Critical or High severity defects can proceed to UAT
  • Conduct regression testing after each fix batch
  • Prepare the UAT environment, test data, and UAT test scripts for the client
  • Get internal QA sign-off before opening UAT
Key Deliverables
  • QA test results report — pass/fail per acceptance criterion
  • Defect log with severity classification and resolution status
  • Internal QA sign-off document
  • UAT test scripts and test data ready for client
  • UAT environment confirmed and accessible
Common Pitfalls
  • ⚠ Opening UAT with unresolved Critical defects — damages client confidence immediately
  • ⚠ Not testing edge cases — they always come up in UAT if missed here
  • ⚠ Not classifying defects against the FRD — leads to disputes over what is in-scope
  • ⚠ Skipping regression testing after a major fix — new bugs introduced silently

08
User Acceptance Testing (UAT)
Objective: Get formal client acceptance that the system meets agreed requirements
UAT Results Defect Resolution UAT Sign-off
UAT is the client's opportunity to validate that the system they approved in the FRD has been built as agreed. The project team's job during UAT is not to explain the system — it is to facilitate testing, classify defects against the FRD, and drive the session toward a clean sign-off. UAT without a sign-off at the end is not UAT — it is an open loop.
What Happens
  • Brief the client team on the UAT process, test scripts, and how to log defects
  • Facilitate UAT sessions — guide testers through the test scripts without influencing outcomes
  • Log all client-raised issues in the defect tracker with full details
  • Classify every defect against the signed FRD: in-scope bug, FRD gap, or new requirement
  • Resolve all in-scope defects and communicate timeline for each fix
  • Raise CRs for any new requirements identified during UAT — do not absorb them silently
  • Run re-test sessions after fix batches — confirm each resolved defect passes
  • Maintain a UAT status tracker shared with the client — full transparency on progress
  • Obtain formal UAT sign-off document from named client approver before proceeding
Key Deliverables
  • UAT defect log — fully classified and resolved
  • UAT status report — shared daily during UAT period
  • CRs for any out-of-scope items raised during UAT
  • Formal UAT sign-off document — signed by named client approver
  • Go/No-Go recommendation from the delivery team
Common Pitfalls
  • ⚠ Allowing new requirements to be raised in UAT without a CR — scope creep at the worst possible time
  • ⚠ Not classifying defects — every unclassified defect is a commercial dispute waiting to happen
  • ⚠ Proceeding to Go Live without a signed UAT document
  • ⚠ Not setting a UAT time limit — open-ended UAT delays Go Live indefinitely

3Use AI to cross-check: "Does every FRD requirement have at least one UAT test case?" — identifies coverage gaps before testing starts.
4Use AI to draft the UAT sign-off document, defect triage process, and go/no-go criteria before UAT begins.
5After UAT, use AI to generate the defect analysis summary and lessons-learned for the project retrospective.

09
Go Live
Objective: Deploy to production safely, with a tested rollback plan and hypercare team in place
Production Deployment Go Live Checklist Hypercare Plan
Go Live is not the end — it is the beginning of the most high-visibility period of the engagement. Everything that was agreed, built, and tested now becomes real for the client's business. A clean Go Live requires more preparation than any other phase. The rollback plan must be tested. The hypercare team must be briefed. Nothing should be improvised.
What Happens
  • Complete the 40-point Go Live readiness checklist — no gaps permitted
  • Confirm all compliance and security requirements have been met and signed off
  • Test the rollback plan in a staging environment before production deployment
  • Brief the hypercare support team — response SLAs, escalation path, severity classification

Business Case Writing
When: Client needs internal approval, budget sign-off, or C-suite justification before the project can proceed
DM · PM · BA
▼
Why This Matters

A project without a signed business case is a project that can be cancelled the moment priorities shift. Your job is to make the case so compelling and the risk of inaction so clear that the decision to proceed becomes obvious. This skill is used by DMs and PMs in pre-sales, by BAs when scoping with new stakeholders, and by POs when justifying new product investment.

The 6-Block Structure
1
Problem Statement — Define the problem in business terms with a quantified cost. Never lead with the solution.
2
Proposed Solution — What will be built or changed. One paragraph. Plain language. No technical jargon.
3
Investment Required — Total cost, timeline, and resource commitment. Be precise — vague numbers lose credibility.
4
ROI Projection — 12, 24, and 36-month return. Payback period clearly stated. Conservative estimates only.
5
Risk of Doing Nothing — Compliance exposure, revenue leakage, competitive disadvantage. This is often the most persuasive block.
6
Recommendation & Next Step — One clear recommendation. One named next action with a deadline. Make it easy to say yes.
Common Mistakes
  • Leading with features instead of the business problem — executives fund outcomes, not features
  • Using technical language in a document intended for business decision-makers
  • Leaving the ROI section vague — "cost savings" without numbers is not a business case
  • Not including the risk of inaction — the most powerful section is often skipped
02
ROI & Value Analysis
When: Budget justification, investment comparison, or demonstrating delivery value to leadership
DM · PM · BA
▼
Why This Matters

Every project Heaptrace delivers must be justified in financial terms. A DM who cannot articulate the ROI of an engagement will lose the renewal conversation. A PM who cannot show delivery value to leadership will not get budget for the next sprint. Understanding how to frame and calculate value is a non-negotiable skill at all levels.

Value Quantification Methods
1
Cost Reduction — Headcount saved, process hours eliminated, infrastructure replaced. Calculate: (Hours saved/week × 52 × hourly cost)
2
Revenue Enablement — New capability enabling new revenue streams. Calculate: (Addressable market × expected conversion × margin)
3
Risk Mitigation — Compliance fines avoided, breach costs reduced, insurance savings. Calculate: (Probability × potential loss)
4
Productivity Gain — Teams doing more with the same headcount. Calculate: (Throughput increase × unit value)
Payback Period Formula

Payback Period = Total Investment ÷ Annual Benefit
ROI % = ((Total Benefit - Total Cost) ÷ Total Cost) × 100

Presentation Rules
  • Always use conservative estimates — credibility is lost the moment actual results fall short of projections
  • Show three scenarios: conservative, realistic, optimistic — never just one number
  • Tie every financial figure to a named source or assumption — not guesses
  • Express ROI in the unit the executive cares about: time, money, headcount, or risk
03
Process Mapping (As-Is → To-Be)
When: Workflow automation, system replacement, or any engagement involving operational change
BA · PO · PM
▼
Why This Matters

You cannot design a better process without fully understanding the broken one. Process mapping surfaces hidden pain points, workarounds, handoff failures, and inefficiencies that stakeholders have normalised. The As-Is map shows the real cost of the status quo. The To-Be map becomes the blueprint for the FRD.

The 4-Step Method
1
Map As-Is — Document every step, decision point, and handoff in the current process. Include who does each step, how long it takes, and what can go wrong.
2
Identify Pain Points — Mark every step that causes delay, rework, manual effort, errors, or frustration. Quantify the cost where possible.
3
Design To-Be — Redesign the process to eliminate pain points. Every removed step or automated handoff is a measurable improvement.
4
Gap Analysis — The difference between As-Is and To-Be becomes your requirements list. This feeds directly into the BRD and FRD.
Key Rules
  • Map the process as it actually works — not as the official documentation says it works
  • Shadow users if possible — what they say they do and what they actually do are often different
  • Every workaround is a requirement in disguise — document them all
  • Validate the To-Be with the people who do the work, not just the managers who oversee it

Vendor Evaluation Framework
When: Client is choosing between vendors, platforms, or evaluating build vs. buy

▼
Why This Matters

The team member who can structure this decision objectively, with a defensible scoring framework, becomes the trusted advisor — not just the delivery partner. This skill is critical for DMs in pre-sales and BAs during discovery on platform-selection engagements.

Evaluation Dimensions
1
Functional Fit — Does the vendor meet the core requirements? Score each requirement as met/partial/not met.
2
Total Cost of Ownership — Licence, implementation, integration, training, and ongoing support. 3-year TCO comparison.
3
Integration Complexity — How hard is it to connect this vendor to the client's existing systems? APIs, data formats, middleware required.
4

Build when: the requirement is a core differentiator for the client's business and no existing solution fits. Buy when: the requirement is a commodity function that multiple vendors solve adequately. The default should always be buy — building bespoke software is expensive and creates long-term maintenance risk.

Commercial Negotiation & Pricing Awareness
When: Budget conversations, rate negotiations, scope pricing, or any commercial discussion with a client
DM · PM
▼
Why This Matters

Pricing conversations are uncomfortable for most delivery professionals — but they are unavoidable. A DM or PM who cannot defend a rate, frame a budget discussion, or handle a client asking for a discount will lose margin on every engagement. This is not sales — it is protecting the value of the work you deliver.

Pricing Conversation Principles
1
Lead with value, not cost — Never open with "it will cost X." Open with "what this will achieve is Y." Cost is the last topic, not the first.
2
Know your floor — Understand the minimum commercially viable rate before the conversation starts. Never negotiate without knowing where you stop.
3
Trade, don't discount — If a client asks for a lower price, reduce scope — do not reduce the rate. Discounting rates devalues the team permanently.
4
Anchor high — Present the full-scope option first. The client will negotiate down from your anchor, not from their budget ceiling.
5
Silence is a tool — After presenting a price, stop talking. The first person to speak after a price quote typically makes a concession.
Fixed Price vs T&M — Know the Difference

Fixed Price: Scope is locked before work starts. Any change requires a CR. Heaptrace carries the risk of scope creep if requirements are not locked. Requires a complete, signed FRD before work begins.

T&M (Time & Materials): Client pays per hour/day of effort. Heaptrace carries less scope risk but must track and justify hours. Requires clear billing milestones and transparent timesheets. Avoid open-ended T&M without a not-to-exceed cap.

Delivery Risk & Commercial Protection
When: Throughout every active delivery — this is an ongoing discipline, not a one-time activity
DM · PM · BA
▼
Why This Matters

Most project overruns are not caused by technical failures — they are caused by commercial failures: scope absorbed without a CR, verbal agreements not documented, payment milestones not tied to deliverables, change requests started before commercial sign-off. Protecting the engagement commercially is every team member's responsibility.

The 5 Commercial Warning Signs
1
Scope Creep Pattern — Three or more verbal additions in one sprint without a CR being raised. This is a commercial emergency, not a planning issue.
2
Payment Milestone Disputes — Client questioning whether a milestone has been reached. Always tie milestones to documented deliverables, not dates.
3
Undocumented Decisions — Key project decisions made verbally without email confirmation. Every decision must have a paper trail.
4
Expanding Stakeholders — New people joining the client side mid-project who were not part of the BRD/FRD process. They will bring new requirements.
5
Sign-off Delays — Client delays on document approvals without explanation. This is often a political sign — escalate before it becomes a project stopper.
The Golden Rule

No work starts without a signed scope document. No scope changes without a signed CR. No milestones paid without a signed acceptance. Every Heaptrace engagement must have a paper trail that could stand up to a commercial dispute. If it is not in writing, it does not exist.

Process Guides

Step-by-step playbooks for the processes every team member must be able to execute. These are not suggestions — they are the Heaptrace standard operating procedures. Follow them, and delivery is consistent and professional. Deviate from them, and you are creating risk.

01
Discovery & Requirements Gathering
The foundation of every successful delivery. Used by BA, PM, and PO at project initiation and for any new feature.
▼
Phase 1 — Prepare
1 Research the client's industry, competitors, and business context before the first session
2 Identify all stakeholders and map them: decision-maker, influencer, subject matter expert, end user
3 Prepare the discovery agenda and share it 24 hours in advance
4 Set up the recording or note-taking approach — never rely on memory
Phase 2 — Discover
5 Open with the business problem — not the solution. "What are you trying to achieve?" not "What do you want to build?"
6 Use the 5-Why technique to get to root causes — the first answer is rarely the real problem
7 Map the current process (As-Is) before designing the future state
8 Document every pain point, workaround, and constraint — these become requirements
9 Ask about non-functional requirements explicitly: performance, security, integrations, compliance
Phase 3 — Document & Validate
10 Send meeting notes within 24 hours — assumptions stated, open questions listed, action items named
11 Write the BRD from the discovery output — business language only, no technical jargon
12 Review the BRD with the client — every requirement confirmed, every assumption validated
13 Get the BRD signed before proceeding to FRD or development
⚠ Watch For
Stakeholders who describe the solution instead of the problem — redirect to the outcome they need
Missing stakeholders — who else will be affected by this? Who else needs to be in the room?
Scope inflation — log everything that comes up but flag what is out of scope immediately
02
Writing a BRD
The primary output of Phase 1. The business contract before the technical contract. Every team member must be able to produce a client-ready BRD.
▼
BRD Structure — 8 Mandatory Sections
1 Executive Summary — Problem statement, proposed solution, and expected business outcomes. 1 page maximum. Decision-maker reads only this.
2 Business Context — Current state, pain points, and the cost of inaction. Quantified where possible.
3 Project Scope — What is included. What is explicitly excluded. Scope boundary must be unambiguous.
4 Business Requirements — What the business needs the system to do. Outcome-focused. No technical language. Numbered for traceability.
5 Stakeholders & Roles — Who owns each requirement. Who approves each section. Who is impacted.
6 Assumptions & Constraints — Every assumption that, if wrong, changes the scope or cost. Every constraint that limits the solution space.
7 Success Criteria — How will we know the project has succeeded? Measurable, time-bound criteria only.
8 Sign-off Page — Named approvers, signature lines, and version control. Without this, the BRD is not valid.
⚠ Common BRD Failures
Technical language in a business document — rewrite in business terms or lose executive buy-in
Requirements without acceptance criteria — how will you prove it is done?
Missing out-of-scope section — this is the most important section commercially
No version control — a BRD without version history is a document waiting to cause a dispute
03
Writing an FRD
Translates business requirements into functional specifications. The development team's contract. Zero ambiguity is the goal.
▼
FRD Structure
1 System Overview — What the system does and how it fits into the client's technology landscape
2 User Roles & Permissions — Every user type, what they can see, what they can do, what they cannot do
3 Functional Requirements — Screen-by-screen or feature-by-feature. Each requirement: FR-XXX, description, priority, acceptance criteria, linked BRD requirement
4 Non-Functional Requirements — Performance (response times), security (auth, encryption), scalability targets, compliance requirements
5 Integration Requirements — Every third-party system, API, or data source. Data flow, format, frequency, error handling
6 Data Requirements — Data model overview, migration requirements, data retention and archival policies
7 Open Questions — Everything not yet confirmed. Named owner, target resolution date. This section must be empty before development starts.
8 Sign-off — Same as BRD. Without a signed FRD, development does not start.
⚠ FRD Quality Gate
Every functional requirement must have testable acceptance criteria — "the system shall allow" is not enough
No open questions before development starts — each one is a scope risk waiting to happen
Every requirement must be traceable to a BRD requirement — nothing in the FRD should be new scope
04
Change Request (CR) Process
The commercial protection mechanism. Every change to signed scope — regardless of size — goes through this process.
▼
CR Workflow — 6 Steps
1 Identify — Any request that adds, removes, or changes signed scope triggers a CR. Log it immediately — do not start work.
2 Document — CR form: what is changing, why, what the original scope said, and what the new requirement is
3 Estimate — Effort and timeline impact. Always provide a range: optimistic, realistic, pessimistic
4 Commercial Pricing — Cost to the client. Align with DM before presenting to client
5 Client Approval — Present the CR, answer questions, get written sign-off. Email confirmation is the minimum standard.
6 Update Scope Documents — BRD and FRD updated, version numbers incremented, new baselines distributed to all stakeholders
⚠ CR Red Lines
Never start out-of-scope work without a signed CR — "We can start and raise the CR later" is how margin disappears
Small CRs are not exempt — a pattern of small unpriced changes is how major scope creep happens
Verbal approval is not approval — email confirmation of CR acceptance is the minimum required

Status Reporting
Weekly status reports are the most visible artefact of delivery quality. A well-written status report builds confidence. A poorly written one creates anxiety.
▼
The Status Report Structure
1 RAG Status — Overall: Green / Amber / Red. Separate RAG for: Scope, Schedule, Budget, Quality. If any is Amber or Red, explain why in one sentence.
2 This Week — What was completed. Bullet points. Quantified where possible. Never "worked on X" — always "completed X" or "delivered X".

Send every Friday — late status reports signal poor discipline before the client reads a word
One A4 page maximum — if it does not fit on one page, you are including too much detail
Never hide bad news — an Amber or Red status with a clear mitigation builds more trust than a false Green
Status reports are a communication tool — they should be readable by an executive in under 2 minutes
07
Escalation Handling
How to handle issues before they become crises — and how to manage a crisis when prevention was not possible.
▼
Escalation Levels
L1 Team-level — Blocker that the delivery team can resolve without client involvement. Raise in standup. Resolve within the sprint.
L2 Client decision required — Blocker or risk that requires a client decision. Raise immediately — do not wait for the weekly status report.
L3 Commercial or relationship risk — Issue with budget, scope, contract terms, or client satisfaction. Escalate to DM immediately. DM owns the response.
L4 Leadership — Formal dispute, contract breach, or significant relationship breakdown. Heaptrace leadership involved. Client executive contact required.
Escalation Message Format
1 Situation — What is happening? One paragraph, factual, no blame.
2 Impact — What will happen if this is not resolved? Timeline, scope, commercial, relationship impact.
3 Options — Two or three options for resolution. Pros and cons of each. Do not present one option and call it a recommendation.
4 Recommendation — Which option do you recommend and why? Own the recommendation — do not make the executive decide without guidance.
5 Decision Required By — When must this be decided to avoid further impact?

Sprint Ceremony Guide
The agile heartbeat of every delivery. Run these ceremonies well and sprints are predictable. Run them poorly and sprints become theatre.
▼
Sprint Planning (Start of Sprint — 2 hrs)
1 Review and confirm backlog priority with the PO — top stories are refined and ready
2 Team commits to stories they can complete — velocity-based, not aspirational
3 Sprint goal agreed and written — one sentence that describes what "done" looks like for this sprint
4 Dependencies and risks called out before sprint starts — not discovered mid-sprint
Daily Stand-Up (Every Day — 15 mins)
5 Three questions only: what did I complete yesterday, what will I complete today, what is blocking me
6 Blockers noted and assigned — not resolved in stand-up, resolved after
7 Sprint board updated before stand-up — not during it
Sprint Review (End of Sprint — 1 hr)
8 Demo only completed stories — do not demo work in progress, even if it is 95% done
9 Client feedback captured and added to the backlog immediately
10 Sprint velocity reported — stories completed vs. stories committed
Retrospective (End of Sprint — 45 mins)
11 What went well — celebrate it, do more of it
12 What did not go well — root cause, not blame
13 One improvement action — named owner, implemented in the next sprint, no more than one at a time
⚠ Ceremony Failure Patterns
Stand-ups that run 45 minutes — they are problem-solving sessions, not stand-ups. Reschedule the discussion.
Sprint reviews without demos — if you cannot demo it, it is not done
Retrospectives that produce no action — a retro without a committed improvement is a waste of time
Sprint planning where the team commits to everything in the backlog — push back on unrealistic sprint loads
09
UAT Management
User Acceptance Testing is the client's final check before go-live. Manage it well and it is a confidence builder. Manage it poorly and it becomes an open-ended risk.
▼
UAT Playbook — 5 Stages
1 UAT Plan — Scope, test scenarios (mapped to FRD requirements), testers named, environment confirmed, timeline set with a hard end date
2 Defect Triage — Every raised defect classified: Critical (P1), Major (P2), Minor (P3), Cosmetic (P4). Only P1 and P2 block go-live.
3 Daily UAT Stand-Up — Progress against test scenarios, new defects raised, defects resolved. Keep momentum — UAT cannot drift.
4 UAT Sign-Off Gate — Zero P1/P2 defects open. All P3/P4 logged as post-go-live items with agreed timelines. Client sign-off in writing.
5 Go-Live Clearance — UAT sign-off document co-signed by client and Heaptrace PM/DM. This document authorises go-live.
⚠ UAT Risk Management
UAT that starts without a defined end date becomes a never-ending extension — set the date upfront and hold it
Client testers who are too busy to test — escalate to sponsor, not to the tester. This is a resource risk that must be resolved above the team level.
New requirements raised during UAT — these are CRs, not defects. Classify and handle through the CR process.
10
Go-Live & Handover
The final phase of every delivery. How you close an engagement determines whether you get the renewal. Do not underinvest in it.
▼
Go-Live Checklist
1 UAT sign-off document received and filed
2 Go-live runbook prepared — step-by-step deployment plan with rollback procedure
3 Hypercare period defined — typically 2 weeks of elevated support post-go-live
4 Training completed and materials handed over
5 All documentation (BRD, FRD, technical docs) handed over in final versions
6 Access and credentials transferred to client — Heaptrace access revoked unless support is contracted
7 Project closure meeting — what was delivered, lessons learned, outstanding items tracked
8 Phase 2 conversation initiated — the best time to discuss the next engagement is when the client is happiest
⚠ Handover Essentials
Never go-live on a Friday — if something breaks, you have no weekend support capacity
Always have a rollback plan — documented, tested, and understood by the team before the go-live window opens
Collect client feedback at closure — a formal satisfaction survey or conversation. Use it to improve the next engagement and to build the case study.

Section 08

Risk Analysis

Risk management is not a document you write at the start of a project and forget. It is a live discipline practised by every team member throughout the engagement. The team that identifies risks early and mitigates them proactively is the team that delivers on time, on budget, and without surprises.

Most project failures are not caused by technical problems — they are caused by risks that were visible weeks earlier and not acted upon. The risk register exists so that known risks cannot be ignored. Every risk must have a probability, an impact, an owner, and a mitigation. Without these four elements, it is not a risk — it is a worry.

▶ Risk Scoring — How to Rate Every Risk

Probability
Low Unlikely to occur — less than 30% chance
Medium Possible — 30–60% chance
High Likely to occur — more than 60% chance
Impact
Low Minor delay or cost — manageable within the team
Medium Impacts timeline, budget, or stakeholder satisfaction
High Project-threatening — requires immediate escalation
Response Types
Avoid Change the plan to eliminate the risk entirely
Mitigate Take action to reduce probability or impact
Accept Monitor — the cost of mitigation exceeds the risk
Transfer Pass the risk to the client, vendor, or insurer

A team member allocated to more than one active project simultaneously must flag the conflict immediately — not manage it quietly until commitments slip on both. Resource conflicts are the DM's problem to solve. Surface them.
Don't skip the retrospective when the sprint went badly
The retrospective is most valuable after a difficult sprint. Cancelling or rushing it because everyone is stressed is exactly backwards. The team that reflects after a hard sprint improves. The team that skips it repeats the pattern.

✓ Do's — Using AI Right
01
Always review AI output before it leaves Heaptrace. Every document, requirement, and user story generated by AI must be reviewed by a human BA before being sent to a client. AI produces drafts. You produce the final deliverable.
02
Feed AI rich context. The quality of AI output is directly proportional to the quality of your input. Always include client name, industry, business goal, and any constraints before running any skill.
03
Use AI to challenge your own requirements. After writing requirements, ask AI: "What is missing? What is ambiguous? What edge cases have I not covered?" Use AI as your internal reviewer.
04
Use AI for consistency, not just speed. The primary value of AI at Heaptrace is that every BA produces the same quality output. Use AI to enforce your own standards — same format, same depth, every time.
05
Use AI to prepare for client calls. Before every discovery call, run the brief through ba-requirements-engine to generate clarifying questions. Walk in prepared — never blank.
06
Use AI to catch compliance gaps early. Run every FRD through compliance-manager before sending for sign-off. Compliance discovered in Phase 3 costs hours. Discovered in Sprint 8, it costs weeks.
07
Let AI do the formatting; you do the thinking. AI excels at structure, format, and completeness checks. You bring the business judgment, stakeholder knowledge, and contextual decisions. Divide the labour correctly.
08
Use only Heaptrace-approved AI tools for client data. Never paste client names, financial data, patient records, or PII into public AI tools. Use only the Heaptrace Skills environment for any client-sensitive content.
09
Document which AI tools you used in your deliverable. Note in the document footer or version history which skills were used. This creates an audit trail and helps the team learn what works.
10
Build your own knowledge first, then use AI to accelerate it. AI cannot replace domain expertise — it amplifies it. Understand the client's industry, regulations, and business model before running any skill. The BA who knows the domain and uses AI will always outperform one who relies on AI alone.
✗ Don'ts — AI Mistakes That Cost Projects
01
Never send AI-generated documents to clients without review. AI output is a first draft. Sending it unreviewed is a professional risk. One wrong assumption in an AI-generated BRD that reaches the client becomes a contractual problem.
02
Never trust AI for compliance decisions. AI flags compliance risks — it does not make compliance decisions. If the compliance-manager skill raises a HIPAA or GDPR concern, escalate to the Lead BA. Never resolve a compliance flag yourself without review.
03
Never use AI to replace stakeholder conversations. AI can help you prepare for a discovery call. It cannot replace one. Requirements gathered only through AI prompts — without real client conversations — will miss context, politics, and unspoken constraints that only humans can surface.
04
Never skip sign-off because AI validated the document. AI validation is an internal quality check. It does not substitute for written client sign-off. Requirements go to dev only after a human approver signs off — AI approval is not equivalent.
05
Never let AI make scope decisions. When a client requests a change, AI can help you assess the impact — but the scope decision (absorb, flag, or change request) belongs to the BA and Delivery Manager. Never let an AI output drive a commercial decision.
06
Never accept AI acceptance criteria without testing them mentally. Read every AI-generated acceptance criterion and ask: "Can I physically test this? Is the expected outcome unambiguous?" If you cannot answer yes to both, rewrite it before it goes to a developer.
07
Never blame AI if requirements are wrong. You own the output. If an AI-generated requirement is incorrect and it reaches development, that is a BA quality failure — not a tool failure. The review step is your responsibility.
08
Never use AI to write requirements you do not understand yourself. If you cannot explain a requirement without AI, you should not be the BA on that requirement. Use AI to accelerate understanding — not to bypass it.
09
Never paste the same prompt twice expecting a different result. If AI output is not good enough, improve your input — add more context, be more specific, clarify the domain. Repeating a bad prompt produces a bad result at speed.
10
Never use AI adoption as a metric in client conversations. Clients hire Heaptrace for quality outcomes — not for AI usage statistics. The value of AI is in the output quality and delivery speed. Never lead a client conversation with "we use AI" — lead with "here is what we delivered."

The 20 Named Anti-Patterns

Named failure modes that appear on real projects. If a colleague tells you you're doing one of these, you should know exactly what that means.

The Ghost Agreement
Treating verbal approvals as binding without a paper trail. "They said yes on the call" is a memory — and memories are disputed at the worst possible moment.
The Invisible Scope Add
Absorbing a client request without a CR. "It's just a small thing" is how fixed-price projects become loss-making. There is no such thing as a free scope addition.
The Assumption Burial
Not listing assumptions in the BRD or FRD. Every ambiguity must be captured as a stated assumption or escalated as an open question. Buried assumptions surface as disputes at delivery.
The Late Compliance Flag
Raising a regulatory concern after development is in progress. Rework at this stage costs 5–20x a pre-FRD compliance review. This is a process failure, not a discovery.
The Feature Whisperer
Sharing requirements verbally with developers without a written FRD or ticket. When interpretations differ — and they will — there is no document to point to.
The Orphaned Ticket
A JIRA story with no ACs, no description, no linked FRD section. A developer should be able to build from the ticket without asking anyone a single question.
The Discovery Illusion
One client workshop treated as complete discovery. Discovery is iterative — one session captures the surface. Real requirements emerge after the client has had time to think.
The AI Passthrough
Sending AI-generated content directly to the client without review. Unreviewed AI output that contains an error is a credibility problem the team owns entirely.
The Missing Owner
Logging a risk, action item, or open question with no named owner. If no one owns it, no one fixes it. Every item in every log must have one name — not a team, not "TBD."
The Silent Delay
Knowing a delivery is at risk and sitting on it for more than 48 hours. That is not caution — it is an active decision to let the project fail quietly while hoping the problem resolves itself.
The Stakeholder Merge
Merging conflicting requirements from two stakeholders into one. The conflict must go to the decision-maker. Do not create a requirement that satisfies neither party.
The Perfection Delay
Holding a BRD or FRD for 3 extra weeks because it is not quite perfect. An 80%-complete document reviewed is better than 100%-complete delivered after the project has already drifted.
The Edge Case Amnesia
Writing ACs only for the happy path. Edge cases are where systems break in production. Error states, boundary conditions, and failure scenarios are the actual work.
The Version Void
Sending updated documents without version numbers or a change log. The client has no idea what changed. Version control on every document is not optional.
The Estimation Gamble
Providing estimates without confidence ranges or stated assumptions. "5 days" with no qualifier is a guess packaged as a commitment. Always provide a range and state what it assumes.
The Empty Backlog
Letting the client backlog drain below 10 items beyond the current sprint. An empty backlog means the team has stopped thinking about the client's future — and the engagement will not renew.
The Jargon Dump
Writing BRDs and FRDs in technical language for business stakeholders. A client who cannot understand their requirements document cannot give meaningful sign-off.
The Integration Blind Spot
Documenting feature logic without the integration boundary. Where does the data come from? What is the API contract? Missing integration detail is a sprint blocker that was entirely predictable.
The Comms Silo
Team members communicating directly with senior client stakeholders without the DM or PM knowing. This bypasses governance and can trigger commitments the delivery team has not agreed to.
The UAT Handoff Escape
Treating UAT as the client's problem and disengaging. The delivery team is present throughout — mapping defects to ACs, triaging severity, ensuring sign-off is clean before go-live is authorised.

Section 11

Glossary

A quick-reference dictionary of every abbreviation, acronym, and concept used across Heaptrace delivery. If you see a term in a client document or internal discussion and aren't 100% certain of its meaning, check here first.

🔍 Tip: Use Ctrl+F / Cmd+F to search within this page.
A
AC
Acceptance Criteria — conditions a user story must satisfy to be considered done. Written as Given/When/Then or as a bulleted checklist. Always present in a well-formed story before sprint planning.
ADR
Architecture Decision Record — a documented record of a significant technical or architectural decision, the context in which it was made, and the trade-offs considered. Lives in the project repository.
AML
Anti-Money Laundering — regulatory framework requiring financial institutions to monitor, detect, and report suspicious transactions. Relevant to any fintech or banking project.
API
Application Programming Interface — a contract defining how two software systems exchange data. The BA must document API requirements including authentication method, request/response format, error handling, and rate limits.
B
BA
Business Analyst — the role responsible for eliciting, documenting, and validating requirements. At Heaptrace this role is not a gatekeeper — it is an active delivery contributor present from presales through UAT.
BRD
Business Requirements Document — the high-level document capturing what the business needs. Covers problem statement, stakeholder needs, functional requirements, non-functional requirements, assumptions, and constraints. Precedes the FRD.
Backlog
The prioritised list of all work yet to be done on a product. A healthy backlog has 10+ items beyond the current sprint, is continuously refined, and is owned by the PO with BA input.
C
CR
Change Request — a formal document requesting a modification to agreed scope. Every CR must state what is changing, why, the effort impact, and the cost impact. Never absorb scope changes verbally.
CI/CD
Continuous Integration / Continuous Delivery — the practice of automatically building, testing, and deploying code changes. The BA should understand the CI/CD pipeline enough to know what "done" means at each gate.
CoD
Cost of Delay — the economic impact of not delivering a feature or product on time. Used in SAFe and advanced prioritisation models to make the urgency of backlog items financially explicit.
D
DM
Delivery Manager — owns the client relationship, sprint health, risk tracking, and stakeholder communication. At Heaptrace the DM and BA work together; the DM does not write requirements but must be able to challenge them.
DoD
Definition of Done — the shared checklist of criteria a story or sprint must meet before it can be called complete. Includes code review, tests passing, documentation, and UAT sign-off as applicable.
DPDP
Digital Personal Data Protection Act — India's primary data privacy law (enacted 2023). Applies to any product processing personal data of Indian citizens. Similar to GDPR in principle but with India-specific provisions.
E — F
Epic
A large body of work that can be broken into multiple user stories. Epics are typically too large to complete in a single sprint. At Heaptrace, Epics map to JIRA with a structured description, owner, and acceptance criteria.
FHIR
Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources — the international standard for exchanging electronic health records. Version R4 is the current stable standard. Any healthcare integration project must reference FHIR compliance.
FRD
Functional Requirements Document — details how the system must behave to satisfy the business requirements. Contains user stories, data flows, UI descriptions, API contracts, and open questions. Follows the BRD.
FR / NFR
Functional Requirement / Non-Functional Requirement — FRs describe what the system does (features, behaviours). NFRs describe how the system performs (performance, security, availability, scalability). Both must appear in any BRD.
G — K
GDPR
General Data Protection Regulation — EU data privacy law. Applies to any product processing data of EU residents, regardless of where the company is located. Key obligations: consent, right to erasure, data portability, breach notification.
HIPAA
Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act — US law governing the privacy and security of Protected Health Information (PHI). Any US healthcare project must consider HIPAA compliance from requirements through deployment.
KYC
Know Your Customer — the process by which a financial institution verifies the identity of its clients. Requires document upload, identity verification, and ongoing monitoring. Common requirement in fintech projects.
M — P
MoSCoW
Must Have / Should Have / Could Have / Won't Have — a prioritisation framework for requirements. Use it when scope is contested or needs client sign-off on what ships in Phase 1 vs later.
MVP
Minimum Viable Product — the smallest version of a product that delivers enough value to satisfy early users and validate assumptions. At Heaptrace, MVP definition is a BA responsibility during presales and Phase 1 planning.
PCI-DSS
Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard — security standards for any organisation that handles credit card data. Heaptrace's position: card data must never touch application servers. Use a compliant payment gateway (Stripe, Adyen, etc.).
PHI / PII
Protected Health Information / Personally Identifiable Information — data that can identify an individual. PHI is health-specific (HIPAA). PII is broader (GDPR, DPDP). Any field in the data model that holds PHI or PII needs special handling.
PM
Project Manager — coordinates delivery timeline, resources, and risk. At Heaptrace the PM and DM roles often overlap on smaller engagements. The key distinction: PM manages the project, DM manages the client relationship.
PO
Product Owner — the person responsible for the product backlog, prioritisation decisions, and accepting or rejecting completed work. May be a client-side role or an internal Heaptrace role depending on the engagement model.
R — S
RACI
Responsible / Accountable / Consulted / Informed — a matrix mapping roles to tasks. Use in kickoff documents to eliminate ambiguity about who owns what decision on a project.
RAID
Risks / Assumptions / Issues / Dependencies — the standard log for tracking project health factors. Updated weekly. Every active project at Heaptrace must have a living RAID log.
RBAC
Role-Based Access Control — controlling system access by user role rather than individual identity. Every application requires a RBAC model; the BA must document all roles, permissions, and permission matrix before development begins.
RFP
Request for Proposal — a client document inviting vendors to submit a proposal for a project. When Heaptrace receives an RFP, the BA should analyse it for hidden requirements, ambiguities, and scope gaps before the proposal is written.
SOC 2
Service Organisation Control 2 — an auditing framework for cloud service providers covering security, availability, processing integrity, confidentiality, and privacy. Enterprise clients often require their vendors to be SOC 2 compliant.
SOW
Statement of Work — the contractual document defining the scope, deliverables, timeline, and commercial terms of an engagement. The BA must be fluent in the SOW — every requirement discussion must be mapped back to it.
T — Z
UAT
User Acceptance Testing — the final validation phase where the client tests the product against agreed acceptance criteria. The BA owns the UAT checklist. UAT sign-off is the gate before Go-Live.
UX / UI
User Experience / User Interface — UX covers the overall user journey and interaction design. UI covers the visual layer. The BA bridges UX requirements into functional specifications that the UI designer and developer can both act on.
WCAG
Web Content Accessibility Guidelines — international standard for web accessibility. Level AA (2.1) is the current minimum for any public-facing or government application. Covers contrast ratios, keyboard navigation, screen reader support, and more.
WBS
Work Breakdown Structure — a hierarchical decomposition of the total scope into deliverable-oriented components. Used in project planning to estimate effort and assign ownership at granular level.
Wireframe
A low-fidelity visual representation of a UI layout. Used by BA and UX to communicate structure before any design tool is opened. At Heaptrace, the wireframe skill produces ASCII wireframes that can be included in FRDs.
